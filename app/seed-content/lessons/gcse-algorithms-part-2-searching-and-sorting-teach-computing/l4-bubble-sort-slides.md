# Bubble sort

## Today we are learning
- say why computers need to sort data
- do one pass of a bubble sort, swapping items that are out of order
- carry out a full bubble sort

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the "I can…" aloud. Vocabulary on board: bubble sort · pass · compare · swap. Avoid the phrase "bubbling up" — the largest item actually sinks to the end.

## Starter — finding a surname
Two copies of the same names: one jumbled, one in order. Which is quicker to search?

> 🧑‍🏫 The ordered copy. Draw out the key theme of this block: we sort data to make searching faster.

## Bubble sort — compare neighbours  (I do)
Compare two items next to each other. If they are in the wrong order, swap them. Move along the list.

> 🧑‍🏫 Step through the card picture. One pass = all the way along once. After one pass, the largest item is at the end.

## One pass on cards
![One pass of a bubble sort: each step compares two cards and swaps if needed; the biggest ends at the right]({{res:l4-one-pass-of-a-bubble-sort-teach-computing.png}})

Watch how the biggest card moves to the end after one pass.

> 🧑‍🏫 Point out a step where NO swap happens (already in order). Number of comparisons in a pass = number of items − 1.

## Your turn — one pass  (we do)
On the worksheet, order the steps, then label the picture: a swap, a no-swap, and the biggest card.

> 🧑‍🏫 Support: the drag-to-order steps. Core: comparisons-per-pass. Challenge: label task + why one fewer pair each pass. Likely error: comparing items far apart — fix-words "only ever the two next to each other."

## Full bubble sort  (you do)
Keep doing passes until no swaps are needed. Then the whole list is in order.

> 🧑‍🏫 They do a full sort of the cuisines list and screenshot it. After each pass, the next biggest is in place, so you can check one fewer pair.

## I can…
Tick your four "I can…". Tell me which item is sorted after the first pass.

> 🧑‍🏫 Answer: the biggest, at the end. Note who completed a full sort. Bubble sort is simple but slow — a stepping stone to better sorts.
