# The credibility of sources

## Today we are learning
- not all information online is reliable
- use checks to judge a source
- decide if a source is credible and say why
- choose a cause to support

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. Vocabulary: credibility · source · audience.

## Starter — real or fake?
Anyone can put information on the internet.

![A newspaper with the headline "The Daily Fake News"]({{res:l3-fake-news-newspaper.png}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Use the starter worksheet. Show a story and ask who thought it was real or fake. The "fifteen sheep at a French school" story is actually TRUE — we can check the real town website. Point: check before you decide.

## Who can put information online?  (I do)
Anyone — so we must check it. Example: Wikipedia can be edited by anyone.

> 🧑‍🏫 Discuss how Wikipedia is edited. Likely error: "Wikipedia is always right / always wrong" → fix-words: "we check who wrote it and where the facts come from."

## Four checks  (we do)
Who wrote it? When? Does anywhere else say the same? Are there links to other sources?

![Question words: who, when, where, how, why, what]({{res:l3-credibility-questions.png}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Walk through the four checks. These match the activity worksheet multi-select. Support: tick the checks. Challenge: a recent date but no author — still trust it?

## Spot the clues  (you do)
Write a short real or fake story; a partner hunts for the clues.

> 🧑‍🏫 10 minutes to write, then swap and race to find the clues. Keep it calm; this is about checking, not tricking.

## Your cause
Think of a cause you care about for your blog project.

> 🧑‍🏫 Think · pair · share. Cohort-friendly examples: looking after the local park, recycling at school, kindness to animals. Note who has a clear cause.

Tick your four "I can…". Tell me one check you will use.
