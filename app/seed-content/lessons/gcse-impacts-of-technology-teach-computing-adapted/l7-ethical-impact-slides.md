# Ethical impact

## Today we are learning
- explain what "ethical" means
- explain the ethical issues around AI
- explain how algorithms can be biased

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the "I can…" aloud. Vocabulary: ethics · artificial intelligence · algorithm · bias. Keep dilemmas calm and respectful — there is no single right answer.

## Starter — ethical questions
Ethical questions are about right and wrong. They often use the word "should".

> 🧑‍🏫 The point of a dilemma is to question it and examine our own thinking — not to "win". This activity is for discussion, not marking.

## What does "ethical" mean?
- **ethics** = considerations about right and wrong, morality and power
- ethics often overlaps with the other impacts (legal, privacy, cultural)

> 🧑‍🏫 Example overlap: keeping data secure is both ethical AND privacy. Draw the link out loud.

The Moral Machine project studies how a car should "choose" in a no-win crash.

> 🧑‍🏫 Hook video "What is AI?" (teacher-played; first 3 minutes) — AI learns two ways: symbolic reasoning and machine learning. Then try one Moral Machine scenario together. Some pupils may opt out of the harsher scenarios — that is fine.

## Algorithms make decisions
- algorithms now choose what we see and help make big decisions
- the person who writes the rules has power over the result

> 🧑‍🏫 Search engines and news feeds are run by algorithms — that is a lot of control over what we find and feel.

## Algorithms can be biased
- bias can come from the **rules** a person writes or the **data** used
- example: a police "gangs matrix" was accused of racial bias

> 🧑‍🏫 Worked example (I do): a face-recognition system trained mostly on one group works worse on others. We do: where does bias come from? You-do: "tick all the sources of bias." Likely error: "the screen colour" ticked → fix-words "bias comes from the rules and the DATA, not the hardware."
