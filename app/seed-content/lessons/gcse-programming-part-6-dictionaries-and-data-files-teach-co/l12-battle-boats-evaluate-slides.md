- explain why evaluation matters
- evaluate my project against the success criteria
- describe my strengths and what I'd do differently
- submit my finished project

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. Final lesson of the project and of the unit. Vocabulary on the board: evaluate. The whole project is the summative assessment — mark it with the Battle Boats rubric.

## Starter — why evaluate?
Why is it useful to evaluate a project when it is finished?

> 🧑‍🏫 Think–pair–share. Evaluation = taking stock: did I meet the criteria, what was hard, what would I change. Support: tick "compare against success criteria."

## Evaluate against the scenario  (I do)
- Go task by task: did I meet it? What's my evidence?
- Be honest about **strengths AND weaknesses**.
- The rubric also looks at: naming conventions, subroutines, commenting, validation, testing, how user-friendly it is.

> 🧑‍🏫 I-do. Model evaluating one task honestly. Remind them "partly" is fine — evidence matters more than ticks. This is the Evaluation row of the rubric.

## Your turn  (you do)
- Evaluate each task (yes / partly / no, with a reason).
- Core: summarise strengths and weaknesses.
- Challenge: which explorer task you'd add next, and how.
- Then the **unit quiz** — the whole unit's check.

> 🧑‍🏫 You-do. After the evaluation, set the **Dictionaries and data files unit quiz** (key words, dictionary syntax, file modes, reading a file, CSV split/join, write code, confidence). This is the unit's final knowledge check alongside the project. TA: prompt, don't answer for them.

## Submit your project
Submit your program, design, success criteria, testing table, and evaluation.

> 🧑‍🏫 Plenary. Collect the full project for marking against the rubric (printout or electronic). Celebrate finishing a demanding project. Movement break is routine.
