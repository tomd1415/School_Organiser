# Script kiddies — hacking, DDoS & the law

## Today we are learning
- define hacking in cybersecurity
- explain how a DDoS attack affects users
- name ways to make a brute force attack harder
- explain why we need the Computer Misuse Act (1990)

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. The "hack George's account" starter is a FICTIONAL scenario — never reframe it onto a real pupil.

## Starter — what is hacking?
Hacking in cybersecurity = gaining access to a system **without permission** ("unauthorised").

> 🧑‍🏫 Stress the word "unauthorised". Ethical hackers (penetration testers) DO have permission. Take a quick vote on whether the starter was ethical.

## How a DDoS attack works  (I do → we do)
![A DDoS attack card]({{res:l3-attack-ddos-card.jpg}})

Many computers flood one server with requests until it cannot cope.

> 🧑‍🏫 Optional paper-ball demo: one client vs many. Then pupils put the DDoS steps in order on the worksheet. Real users can't reach the site.

## Brute force & strong passwords  (you do)
![A brute force attack card]({{res:l3-attack-brute-force-card.jpg}})

A brute force attack tries password after password. Long + mixed = much harder.

> 🧑‍🏫 Card sort weak vs strong, then tick the rules that help. Locking the account after a few tries also helps.

## The Computer Misuse Act (1990)  (you do)
![A hacking attack card]({{res:l3-attack-hacking-card.jpg}})

Three sections: 1) unauthorised access · 2) access to commit a further crime · 3) impairing a computer.

> 🧑‍🏫 Pupils match each action to a section (or "does not break the Act"). Reading an unlocked colleague's email is wrong, but does NOT break this Act — no security was bypassed.
