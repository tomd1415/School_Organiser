# Good vibrations

## Today we are learning
- sound is a wave
- what a microphone and a speaker do
- the words sample, sampling rate, sample size
- how a sound is stored as bits

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. These words are technical — repeat them often.

## Sound is a wave
A vibrating object pushes the air. The pushing travels out as a **wave**.

![A sound shown as a wave]({{res:l4-sound-wave.png}})

[▶ Play a pure tone]({{res:l4-pure-tone.wav}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Teacher-played hook: play the short pure tone, show the wave. Wave the palm back and forth to show vibration. No flashing visuals.

## Microphone and speaker
- a **microphone** turns sound into electricity (it captures)
- a **speaker** turns electricity into sound (it plays)

![A speaker pushing out sound waves]({{res:l4-speaker-sound-waves.png}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Key idea: devices do not handle sound directly — they handle electricity, then numbers.

## Taking samples
The computer measures the wave many times a second. Each measurement is a **sample**.

![Concept map: sound → samples → sampling rate and sample size]({{res:l4-pcm-sound-concept-map.jpg}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Build the idea slowly: measure → store the number → do it again, fast. This is "sampling".

## Three words to keep
- **sample** = one measurement of the wave
- **sampling rate** = how many samples each second
- **sample size** = how many bits for each sample

> 🧑‍🏫 Likely error: mixing up sampling rate (per second) with sample size (bits per sample). Use the matching task on the worksheet.
