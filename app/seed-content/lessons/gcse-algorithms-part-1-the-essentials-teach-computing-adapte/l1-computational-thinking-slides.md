# Computational thinking

## Today we are learning
- name the three computational thinking skills
- say what decomposition and abstraction mean
- use the skills to help plan a game

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. Same routine as every lesson. Vocabulary on board: decomposition · abstraction · algorithmic thinking · algorithm.

## Starter — five sticks
![Four shapes made from sticks, labelled A, B, C and D]({{res:l1-five-sticks-starter-teach-computing.png}})

Which shape can you make by moving just ONE stick?

> 🧑‍🏫 Think · pair · share, then class discussion. The point: solving problems needs a mix of skills. We revisit this in the plenary. Do not reveal the answer yet — let pairs explain how they checked.

## What is computational thinking?  (I do)
A step-by-step way to solve a problem. Three skills: decomposition, abstraction, algorithmic thinking.

> 🧑‍🏫 Stress: this is used by people, with or without a computer. We use all three later for searching and sorting.

## Decomposition — break it down
Split a big problem into smaller problems that are easier to solve.

> 🧑‍🏫 Example: planning a trip. Keep learners on the QUESTIONS to find out, not the solution. A structure diagram shows the parts.

## Abstraction — hide what you do not need
![A street map of part of Paris]({{res:l1-paris-street-map-abstraction-teach-computing.png}})

Keep only the details that matter for the problem.

> 🧑‍🏫 A street map vs an underground map — different details for different jobs. Likely error: deleting needed detail → fix-words "keep what helps THIS problem."

## Algorithmic thinking — ordered steps  (we do)
An algorithm is a set of ordered steps that solve a problem.

> 🧑‍🏫 Numbered steps, done in order. Predict the next step together. These could be broken down further.

## Your turn — plan the game  (you do)
Use the activity worksheet: group the actions, simplify the map, order the route.

> 🧑‍🏫 Support: word-bank/tick boxes. Core: explain choices. Challenge: justify keep/remove. TA: prompt, do not do it for them.
