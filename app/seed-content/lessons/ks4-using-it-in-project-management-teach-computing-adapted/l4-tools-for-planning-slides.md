# Tools for planning

## Today we are learning
- what a Gantt chart shows
- what a PERT chart shows
- how to label the parts of a PERT chart
- how to choose a planning tool

> 🧑‍🏫 Read the four "I can…" aloud. Vocabulary on the board: dependency · concurrent · contingency · milestone. Same routine as every lesson.

## Why add time to a plan?
- so you know if the project will finish on time
- so you can spot tasks running late

> 🧑‍🏫 Starter discussion: "How do you know the project will be done by the end date?"

## Gantt chart
![A Gantt chart drawn as bars]({{res:l4-a-gantt-chart-drawn-as-bars-teach-computing.png}})

- each task is a **bar** across time
- longer bar = more time
- shows milestones and contingency (buffer time)

> 🧑‍🏫 Gantt charts are good for short, simple projects — easy to read once done.

## A real Gantt chart
![A real printed Gantt chart]({{res:l4-a-real-printed-gantt-chart-teach-computing.png}})

> 🧑‍🏫 Same idea on paper: tasks down the side, time across the top.

## PERT chart
![A PERT chart with nodes and arrows]({{res:l4-a-pert-chart-with-nodes-and-arrows-teach-computing.png}})

- circles (**nodes**) = tasks
- **arrows** = the order, with time in minutes
- good for large projects

> 🧑‍🏫 Likely error: drawing arrows in the wrong direction. Fix-words: "arrows point the way the work flows." Pupils label this chart on the worksheet.

## Choose a tool
- short, simple project → Gantt chart
- large project, lots of order → PERT chart

> 🧑‍🏫 Pupils justify their choice on the worksheet, then screenshot their plan.

## I can…
Tick your four "I can…". Point to a node and an arrow on the PERT chart.

> 🧑‍🏫 Note who can explain why they picked their tool.
